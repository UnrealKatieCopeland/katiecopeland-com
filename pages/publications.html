Forthcoming Book

The Invisible Barrier

A manifesto exposing how America's state courts systematically fail to comply with ADA Title II — and a practical framework for holding them accountable. The Invisible Barrier documents the infrastructure of non-compliance: the missing ADA coordinators, the absent self-evaluations, the accommodation requests that disappear into silence.

This is not a book about individual bad actors. It is a book about systems that fail before the first request is ever made — and about the legal tools that already exist to fix them.

ABA Publication · March 2026 Featured

Access Is Not Advantage

American Bar Association — Commission on Disability Rights

A foundational argument published in the ABA journal that reframes how the legal profession understands disability accommodations. The article establishes that providing access to people with disabilities in court proceedings is not granting an advantage — it is meeting a legal obligation that already exists under ADA Title II. The piece challenges the pervasive but unexamined assumption that accommodations give disabled litigants an unfair edge, demonstrating instead that they merely level a playing field that was never level to begin with.

Published Article

The Last Acceptable Discrimination: How America's Courts Strip Disabled Parents of Their Children

An unflinching examination of how family courts across America use disability as a proxy for parental unfitness — stripping custody from parents whose only "failure" is having a disability. The article documents the pattern of courts conflating disability with incapacity, applying heightened scrutiny to disabled parents that would be impermissible in any other civil rights context, and the devastating consequences for families caught in this system.

Original Framework

The Copeland Questions™

Four threshold questions for identifying ADA Title II violations in court proceedings.

The Copeland Questions™ framework is built on a simple premise: courts are already legally required under ADA Title II to provide meaningful access to people with disabilities. The framework doesn't ask courts to do anything new — it asks whether they did what the law already requires.

By asking four threshold questions, any attorney, advocate, or litigant can identify exactly where a court’s ADA Title II obligation was violated — and build the legal record to prove it.

1

Is there a functional barrier?

Does the person’s disability create a barrier to meaningful participation in the court proceeding?

2

Is an accommodation needed?

Would a reasonable modification or auxiliary aid remove the barrier and enable equal access?

3

Has the interactive process been followed?

Did the court engage in a good-faith, individualized dialogue with the person to identify an effective accommodation?

4

Is the denial documented?

If the accommodation was denied or inadequate, is there a written record of the decision and its rationale?

The power of this framework is its simplicity and its legal grounding. Each question maps directly to existing federal requirements. When a court cannot answer these questions affirmatively, the record speaks for itself.

The Copeland Questions™ are designed to serve as a foundation for ADA compliance audits, judicial education programs, CLE presentations, and individual case analysis.

Legislative Proposal

The Family Court Access Act of 2026

A legislative framework for ensuring ADA compliance in family court proceedings.

The Family Court Access Act of 2026 is a legislative proposal drafted by Katie Copeland that addresses the specific and acute failures of family courts to comply with ADA Title II. Family courts present unique challenges: the stakes are among the highest in any legal proceeding — custody of children, parental rights, family integrity — yet these courts often have the weakest infrastructure for disability access.

The proposed legislation would establish clear requirements for family courts to maintain ADA compliance infrastructure, create standardized accommodation request processes, prohibit the use of disability status as a negative factor in custody determinations, and provide enforcement mechanisms for families whose rights are violated.

This proposal builds directly on the analysis in The Invisible Barrier and the diagnostic framework of The Copeland Questions™, translating scholarly analysis into legislative action.
