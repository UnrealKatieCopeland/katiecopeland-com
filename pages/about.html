About Katie

The Both/And of This Work

Legal scholar and lived-experience advocate. Rigorous and human. Systemic thinker and storyteller.

Who I Am

I am a disability rights attorney, author, and advocate based in Texas. My work focuses on one of the most consequential — and least examined — civil rights failures in America: the systematic non-compliance of state courts with the Americans with Disabilities Act.

I came to this work the way many advocates do — not through abstract interest, but through experience. I have navigated the legal system as both an attorney and as a person with a disability. I know what it means to walk into a courtroom and realize that the institution charged with delivering justice has not built the infrastructure to include you in it.

That experience didn't make me angry. It made me precise.

"I don't ask courts to be sympathetic. I ask them to be compliant. The law already requires meaningful access — the question is whether courts are providing it."

The Work I Do

My scholarship and advocacy operate at the systems level. I am not interested in individual grievances — I am interested in the patterns that produce them. When a court lacks a functioning ADA coordinator, when there is no self-evaluation on file, when opposing counsel interferes with accommodation requests without consequence — these are not isolated failures. They are infrastructure problems.

I developed The Copeland Questions™ — a framework of eight threshold questions — to give attorneys, advocates, and litigants a practical tool for identifying exactly where a court's ADA Title II obligations were violated. The framework doesn't require courts to believe anyone. It requires them to examine what they knew, what they asked, what they did, and what they documented.

Courts need to mistake less, not believe more.

The Both/And

I believe in holding complexity. My work is both legally rigorous and deeply personal. I am both a trained litigator and someone who has been on the other side of the bench. I believe courts can protect judicial discretion and meet their legal obligations. That the law can be precise and humane.

This "both/and" framing is not a rhetorical device — it is a philosophical commitment. The world is rarely either/or, and the most important work happens when we refuse to collapse complexity into false binaries.

I am influenced by the work of Brené Brown on wholehearted living and the courage it takes to show up fully in spaces that were not designed for you. My writing aims to be calm, authoritative, and constructive — not because the stakes are low, but because the stakes are too high for anything less.

What Drives Me

I write because the patterns I see in courtrooms across America are not accidents. They are the predictable consequences of systems that have never been required to examine themselves. My book, The Invisible Barrier, is a manifesto about these failures — and a roadmap for fixing them.

I speak because attorneys, judges, and court administrators need practical tools, not just principles. My published work in the American Bar Association journal, my legislative proposals, and my speaking engagements are all designed to translate complex legal doctrine into actionable change.

I advocate because access is not advantage. It is the baseline the law already requires. And until every courtroom in America meets that baseline, this work continues.

Guiding Principles

What I Believe

Access Is a Legal Requirement

Disability accommodations in courts are not favors, advantages, or acts of compassion. They are obligations under federal law. Full stop.

Systems, Not Symptoms

Individual failures are symptoms of systemic non-compliance. Real change requires examining the infrastructure — the policies, training, and processes that courts have or have not built.

Precision Over Grievance

The most effective advocacy is calm, documented, and legally precise. Courts respond to evidence and analysis, not to anger.

Both/And Thinking

The world is rarely either/or. Courts can maintain authority and provide access. Advocacy can be rigorous and compassionate. Complexity is not the enemy — it is the terrain.
